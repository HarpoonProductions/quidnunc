DRAGON OVERBOARD

      An epic tale of lost toys and ocean pollution

      Every day, on beaches around the Cornish coast in the south of England, pieces of Lego wash ashore, tangling with seaweed and sticking to the sand.

      Item 1 of 2

      Many of these Lego pieces are themed around the sea. So there are flippers and scuba diving tanks, spear guns, ships rigging and cutlasses, and tall tendrils of plastic seagrass, all coming ashore with the tides.

      Rarely found, but highly prized are the black octopuses who sneak ashore, washed up by the waves.

      There may be dragons among the haul, too, green and black ones.

      But where are they all coming from, and how did they get in the sea?

      The story begins one stormy night on 13 February 1997, far out to sea.

      A container ship called Tokio Express was being battered by brutal waves and was hit by one so large that its captain described it as a "once in a 100-year phenomenon".

      This tilted the ship 60 degrees one way...

      and then 40 degrees back the other...

      which led to 62 containers being dropped into the ocean about 20 miles from Lands End in Cornwall.

      Image captions

        One of them was carrying boxes of Lego pieces nearly 4.8m pieces, to be precise, which were being shipped to America.

        That container was never seen again, but a short time later some of these Lego pieces began to be found along the coasts of Cornwall.

        Theyre still coming ashore today.

        Lego says the Tokio Express incident was "very unfortunate" but ultimately had nothing to do with the Lego Group, as its items were in the hands of the shipping company.

        The toy maker did add that it continues to have an "overall concern for the environment" and was working to eliminate its production site waste, to prevent plastic litter from entering the sea.

        Oceanographer Curtis Ebbesmeyer has previously tracked other items lost at sea over the years, monitoring where the currents have carried them.

        When he heard about the Great Lego Spill, as this incident became known, he contacted the Lego company to ask for a manifest of what was in that container.

        "The biggest lesson I learned from the Lego story is that things which go to the bottom of the ocean don't always stay there"

        Dr Ebbesmeyer

        Beachcomber Tracey Williams, who lives in Cornwall, knows exactly what he means. Shes spent years collecting lost pieces of Lego now found on her local beaches.

        She started up the Lego Lost at Sea Facebook page, where people can learn more about the story and also share their own finds with the community.

        Tracey has also written a book called Adrift: The Curious Tale of the Lego Lost at Sea.

        Many people use that Facebook page to tell Tracey of their own finds, of flippers and spear guns, scuba tanks and life jackets, along with seagrass and the odd dragon, which have been discovered on various shores over the years.

        Apart from all around the southwest coast of England, Lego has been found washed up in Pembrokeshire and the Gower Peninsula in Wales, Kerry in Ireland and Texel in the Netherlands.

        Someone even took a black octopus, like this one found in the UK, to a beachcombers event in Florida and said it had been discovered on Galveston Island in Texas.

          Of course, all this plastic in the ocean is a disaster for the environment.

          Its estimated that plastic waste comes to 80% of all marine pollution, and between eight to 10 million metric tons of plastic end up in the ocean each year.

          Research also suggests that plastic may outweigh all the fish in the sea by 2050.

          So a spill of nearly 4m pieces of Lego, many of which have escaped from the container still lurking beneath the waves, only adds to that considerable and serious problem.

          We recognise these small pieces of plastic as being tiny flippers, or life jackets, or cutlasses, meant for children to play with as toys.

          But marine life, such as fish and birds, will mistake these small pieces of plastic for food and can end up dying if they ingest them.

          When shes not busy tracking all the Lego which keeps on washing up in tiny but indisputable numbers, day after day, Tracey also cleans the beach of Cornwalls Perran Sands.

          She describes beach cleaning as a "never-ending job because of all the debris in the sea, much of it plastic, is continuing to fill up the oceans and our beaches.

          Despite her concerns for the environment, Tracey does still get satisfaction from finding a piece of the Lego spill.

          "You often find a lot of Lego when you're bending down with a litter-picker with the intention of picking up something else, she says.

          It's like a little reward for picking up the rubbish."
